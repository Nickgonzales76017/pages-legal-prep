## Transcript
Since Lynn and Jim couldn't settle things during mediation, the case moves forward  and a hearing date is set. To get an idea of what to expect in her own case, Lynn sits in on some small claims hearings. She then prepares all her documents in advance, just as she did for mediation, except now she  organizes her documents into four tabbed binders. One for her, one for monster repairs, ink, one for the judge, and one to submit for the court's record. She also reaches out to witnesses, people who saw what happened and can clearly explain the sequence of events. For example, the mechanics assistant witnessed the dispute but says he is not willing to testify. Lynn can ask the court to issue a subpoena, an order that will compel the assistant to participate in the hearing. At the hearing, the plaintiff usually presents her case first, the judge asks questions to both sides. It's important to be direct, clear, and respectful when answering the judge's questions. Never interrupt the judge or the other side when they are speaking. After the judge has heard both sides and asked questions, she will decide the case and issue a judgement. If a judge rules against Lynn, she can appeal to the district court. This is not a new hearing and she cannot present new evidence. It is simply another judge reviewing the evidence and arguments already presented. If the defendant appeals to the district court, he will have to pay a bond of $250 and the amount of any judgement awarded against him. If Lynn appeals, she will only pay the $250 bond. At the end of the case, the judge decides what happens to the bond. There is no guarantee that the person who posted the bond will get it back. [BLANK_AUDIO]
